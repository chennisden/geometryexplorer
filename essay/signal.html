The Handcap Principle

Originally published April [day], 2021.

In biology, the Handicap Principle states that costly signals must be reliable signals, as the signaller pays a price that an individual with less of a trait cannot afford. As an example, peacocks have large tails in order to attract females, despite that making the peacock slower and easier to spot.

In general, there are three requirements:

  1. The two animals communicating must have a reason to decieve each other. (For example, males have reasons to present themselves as more fit than they really are in courting.)
  2. The signal must symbolize a trait adaptive to survival.
  3. The signal must be something that an "inferior" individual cannot replicate because of the costliness.

This applies for humans too. Back in the Victorian Era, obese people were seen as attractive because it was a sign of having enough to eat to become obese. People then still knew obesity made you feel sluggish, but better to be rich and sluggish than poor and starving.

The Handicap Principle is still everywhere in our world. The reason school and grades exist isn't to benefit students. It's to force them to spend time and money to pursue something meaningless. The reason recommendation and reference letters are required is because it takes time to write them.

If people have the ability to survive in a senseless system, they do for a sensible system as well. But wasting people's time is not a good way to measure future success, especially because time isn't the only thing being lost here. When people are measured, the measurement changes them.

Throughout history people have been finding creative ways to defy biology. I have faith the same will happen here.
