Recommendations

Last updated September 9th, 2020.

Math recommendations can be found at Where to Start? This is a list of all other recommendations (more accurately, a list of things that interested me).

Books

Non-fiction

Den of Thieves - A fascinating account of the financial corruption committed by Milken and Boesky, and the ensuing legal battle between the prosecution and the traders.

All the President's Men - Bernstein and Woodward's gripping reconstruction of their initial investigative journalism and how it led to the ultimate downfall of Nixon is a must-read, and it does not hurt that this is a very easy read.

Thinking, Fast and Slow - An interesting exploration of how our minds work, the distinction between the impulsive and more careful and rational side of our minds (System 1 and System 2), and an exploration of some biases and their causes. However, some of the examples are a little exaggerated, with Add-1 being egreigious - adding one digit to a four digit number under a rhythm is nowhere close to most people's mental peaks (any serious academic subject, gaming at a high level, and ostensibly, manuevering several tons of metal at speeds of 60 miles per hour should be much harder).

History's Greatest Hits, and Great Rivals In History - I am clumping these two books together because my commentary on them is the same: These are good history books for young children. History buffs may enjoy this less, but I found it very enthralling as a younger child.

Fiction

Blindness - A harrowing tale of selfishness and surprising bouts of humanity arises when a country is hit with an epidemic of blindness. The writing style, however, is very difficult to parse, so it may not be for everyone.

The Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde - This is a classic that needs little introduction. With its short length, this is a novella that can be knocked out in little over two hours, and is well worth the time to read.

Renegades - This trilogy is a deconstruction of superhero tropes, displaying the selfishness, callousness, and corruptness that is possible in heroic organizations. The villains are also more nuanced; you can't help but root for them a little bit. They are by no means the secret heroes; they are much more selfish and willing to take life than the heroes, which definitely beats out a lot of other "terrorist is secretly hero" type stories (I'm looking at you, Legend). The final book of the trilogy, however, is a massive disappointment.

Scythe - In a post-death world, the only harbringers of death are the Scythes, who dole out death in whatever manner they please. They are theoretically supposed to uphold principles, but with delusions of grandeur and lax enforcement, the consequences of the corruption that grows are inevitable. The commentary on corruption, however, seems to have been forgotten by the author by the final book, as the ending is a huge asspull that doesn't fit with the story.

I, Robot - Isaac Asimov's collection of robot short stories, if described on a surface level, is not the type of book that most outside of a select few sci-fi enthusiasts would be interested in reading. The argument that no action happens in any story can be made easily and with great veracity; indeed, most of the stories can be likened to thought experiments, where the conflict is formed by a given scenario that the characters are put in and the resolution formed by their solution. But this would be a facetious argument and would miss the point of the book. The stories are so appealing because of the simplicity of the scenarios and resolutions. Asimov has enough confidence in his stories not to bluff by introducing complex worlds devoid of substance; he takes his simple premises, explores them, and in doing so, uses his stories touch on a very human aspect of our lives. By using themes from companionship and abandonment in the simple yet heartwarming tale of Gloria to the reverse psychology that the machines in The Evitable Conflict apply to the manipulatable Society, Asimov is able to make us cringe when the grumpy Pollock berates his friend, feel pity for the spoiled Gloria when her robotic friend Robbie is nowhere to be seen, and awed at the magnificence of the simple yet effective planning of Byerly. Though the stories are disjoint, they all feel like part of the same world; instead of feeling like a compilation of disconnected stories, I, Robot instead reads like an epic saga outlining the rise of positronic robots.

Videos

Quantum Computing for Computer Scientists - A very accessible and fascinating talk on quantum computing from a mathematical perspective.

Don't Talk to the Police - A talk by Regent Law Professor James Duane on the various ways talking to the police can help the prosecution convict you, and why you should always plead the fifth.

JavaFX Multiple Controllers - Discusses how to make JavaFX Controllers communicate by injecting one into the other. This was personally crucial to my development of Messier, and I referenced this video several times during it.

6 Problems with our School System - This is somewhat surface level, but this lines up very well with a large portion of my essays.

Anime and Manga

Anime

Psycho-Pass - In a futuristic society, the criminal inclinations of everyone can be measured by a simple scanner. This leads to an extreme form of predictive policing, where the Public Safety Bureau enforce the law and treat people as criminals based on their criminal coefficient.

Death Note - Light Yagami, an idealistic and bored student, gets the Death Note, a book that can kill with just a name and a face. World-class detective L tries to catch Light, who has taken up the alias Kira, and an exciting cat and mouse game ensues.

Steins;Gate - The delusional Okabe Rintarou discovers that he has created a time machine by accident. Steins;Gate follows the consequences of him messing with time and the choices that he was to make. I also recommend Steins;Gate 0 after watching the original, though I offer no commentary on its content for spoiler reasons.

Manga

Yugami-kun Has No Friends - A unique tale about the voluntarily friendless Yugami, his weird idiosyncracies, and his interactions and developing "friendship" with the "normal" Chihiro Watanuki, who is unvoluntarily friendless in her new school.

Liar Game - The naive Nao Kanzaki enters a tournament of deceit, where each round consists of a game to trick their opponents out of money. Each round is psychologically thrilling, the games fascinating (and honestly fun, if the money element were removed), and the actions that the characters take are deeply revealing of the human psyche and nature.

Kaguya-Sama - Two honor students in love with each other are too proud to confess to each other, and a battle of wits and emotions ensues as they try to get each other to confess.

My Home Hero - An ordinary father's discovers that his daughter's boyfriend has abused his daughter, and kills him in fear for her daughter. The manga follows the tale of him and his wife disposing of the body and hiding the truth from the yakuza, and their daughter.

Les Miserables - A stark contrast to reading the book, but worth experiencing nonetheless.

Hamilton - Do I actually need to say anything here?
